What is ISO 12944? Understanding corrosion categories from C1 to CX

Published 12 May 2026 Read time 8 min By VPIC R&D
VPIC INSIGHTS · TECHNICAL STANDARDS

ISO 12944 is the international standard defining how steel structures should be protected from corrosion through coating systems. It is the most-cited standard in every technical specification for projects with steel structures — from warehouses, bridges, and ports to offshore oil rigs. But most people who interact with ISO 12944 remember only the phrase "C5M" without truly understanding what it means.

The current ISO 12944 (2018 edition, 9 parts) defines environments through a corrosivity classification from C1 to CX. Each category corresponds to a different level of severity, requiring different coating systems in terms of layers, dry film thickness (DFT), and binder type.

1. Corrosion categories from C1 to CX

ISO 12944 divides environments into six atmospheric corrosivity categories, measured by the corrosion rate of a reference material (in µm/year for carbon steel).

Category Description Corrosion rate Real-world examples
C1Very low≤ 1.3 µm/yearDry indoor residential, air-conditioned offices
C2Low1.3 – 25 µm/yearDry inland factories, unpolluted
C3Medium25 – 50 µm/yearLight industrial zones, urban, far coast
C4High50 – 80 µm/yearHeavy industrial zones, medium coastal
C5Very high80 – 200 µm/yearCoastal direct exposure, chemical plants, oil rigs
CXExtreme200 – 700 µm/yearOffshore far-from-shore, tropical sheltered coastal

Before the 2018 revision, C5-I (Industrial) and C5-M (Marine) existed as separate sub-categories. The new revision merged them into C5 and introduced CX for the most extreme environments such as North Sea offshore or Gulf of Mexico.

Important note

Vietnam has many areas classified as C4 and C5 due to its tropical humid climate, frequent rainfall, and long coastline. Phu My, Vung Tau, Quang Ninh, Hai Phong — almost all coastal industrial zones — fall into C5. Using a C3 system for a C5 structure is the leading cause of steel structures deteriorating within 3-5 years.

2. Dry film thickness and expected durability

ISO 12944 also defines three durability categories for coating systems — not absolute lifespans, but the time before the first major maintenance:

  • Low (L): 7 years or less — short-term projects
  • Medium (M): 7-15 years — most common for civil and light industrial
  • High (H): 15-25 years — required for bridges, long-term infrastructure
  • Very High (VH): over 25 years — mainly oil and gas, offshore

The higher the durability category, the greater the total dry film thickness (DFT) required. For example, in C5 environment, an M system needs roughly 240 µm DFT while an H system requires 320 µm and VH demands over 400 µm.

Coating system notation

An ISO 12944 coating system is often written as "C5/H — EP/PUR — 320 µm" meaning: for C5 environment, high durability (15-25 years), Epoxy + Polyurethane system, total DFT 320 µm.

3. Typical coating systems per category

A standard ISO 12944 protective coating system typically has three layers:

  1. Primer: In direct contact with steel, providing rust prevention. Common: Zinc-rich Epoxy (80%+ zinc), Zinc-rich Ethyl Silicate.
  2. Intermediate: Builds film thickness, provides barrier resistance. Common: Epoxy MIO (Micaceous Iron Oxide), High-Build Epoxy.
  3. Topcoat: Faces the environment, provides UV and color retention. Common: Aliphatic Polyurethane, Fluoropolymer.
Simple rule: zinc fights rust, epoxy fights moisture, polyurethane fights UV. Each layer solves one problem — skipping any layer breaks the system. Coating Application Handbook — Vietnam Paint Association

Specific example: C5/H system

For a steel structure in a coastal industrial zone, C5 environment, 15-25 year durability target:

LayerProductDFT
PrimerZinc-rich Epoxy 80%75 µm
IntermediateEpoxy MIO150 µm
TopcoatAliphatic Polyurethane50 µm
Total DFT275 µm

4. Three common mistakes applying ISO 12944

Mistake 1: Underestimating the site's corrosion category

Many owners assume a project is "not too close to the sea" and apply a C3 system, when in fact the area may be C4 or C5 due to southeast winds carrying salt aerosols inland. A C3 system on a C5 site degrades within 3-5 years, leading to maintenance costs 3-4 times higher than expected.

Mistake 2: Over-investing in mild environments

Conversely, applying a C5/VH system to a C2 inland warehouse is wasteful. Coating cost doubles while lifespan doesn't improve proportionally — because the limiting factor isn't the coating but the environment.

Mistake 3: Neglecting surface preparation

ISO 12944 Part 4 specifies surface preparation to Sa 2.5 (near-white metal) for most protective systems. Skipping this — applying paint over hand-cleaned surfaces — destroys the value of the entire expensive coating system. This is the most costly mistake but also the most common on Vietnamese construction sites.

In short, ISO 12944 is not a document to display in bidding paperwork. It is a thinking framework helping owners understand their environment, how much to invest, and what lifespan to expect. Applying this standard correctly can save billions of VND in maintenance costs over a project's 20-year lifecycle.
