OEM paint vs own factory: the economics of two models

Published 28 April 2026 Read time 12 min By VPIC R&D
VPIC INSIGHTS · INDUSTRY STRATEGY

A new paint brand is growing 30-50% annually, revenue passing the $2M mark, and now faces the question: continue with OEM contract manufacturing or build its own plant? This decision shapes cash flow for the next 5-10 years and defines the entire operating structure of the company. This article analyzes the question from an economic lens — not from the pride of "having one's own factory".

1. Four common models in Vietnam's paint market

Vietnamese paint brands operate in one of four models:

  • Full OEM: No factory; all products contract-manufactured. 100% focus on brand, channel, and sales.
  • Hybrid (most popular): Small factory for core products, OEM for secondary lines and peak demand. Most mid-sized domestic brands sit here.
  • Full in-house factory: Manufacture everything internally. Only viable at sufficient volume and SKU diversity.
  • Factory + reverse OEM: Own factory, while also OEM-manufacturing for other brands to maximize utilization.

2. The breakeven math for owning a plant

A small paint factory in Vietnam (capacity 2,000-5,000 tons/year) requires an initial investment of $1.2M - $3.2M USD, including:

ItemCost range
Land + workshop (3,000-5,000m²)$600K-$1.2M (buy) / $40K-80K/year (lease)
Machinery (mixers, mills, fillers)$320K-$800K
Fire safety + wastewater treatment$120K-$320K
Lab + QC equipment$40K-$120K
Working capital (materials, AR)$200K-$600K

Annual operating costs

  • Production + QC + engineering staff: $120K-200K/year (15-25 people)
  • Utilities and consumables: $40K-80K/year
  • Equipment depreciation: $60K-120K/year
  • Maintenance + overhead: $30K-60K/year

Total fixed costs around $240K-460K/year before producing a single liter of paint. Compared to OEM where fixed cost is near zero — you only pay when buying.

Key breakeven point

With fixed costs of $240K-460K/year and production margin around 15-25% (difference between own-make cost and OEM purchase price), breakeven sits at approximately 1,500-2,500 tons/year, equivalent to paint revenue of $4-8M. Below this, OEM is strictly cheaper from a pure economics standpoint.

3. Five hidden costs nobody mentions about owning a plant

1. The learning curve cost

In the first year of a new plant, defect rates typically run 5-15% (batches that fail or need rework). This is hard to forecast but can consume $40K-120K in year one. OEM doesn't have this issue because the partner has already paid that tuition.

2. Operational psychological burden

The brand CEO suddenly worries about raw material incidents, workplace injuries, environmental compliance, labor unions, and fire department inspections. This management overhead can't be quantified directly but distracts resources from the main goal: selling paint.

3. Working capital tied up in materials

A factory must keep raw material inventory for continuous production. A 3,000 ton/year plant typically locks up $320K-$600K in materials. That capital could otherwise go to marketing or channel expansion.

4. Inflexibility in changing portfolio

Owning a plant means you must produce enough volume to cover fixed costs — even on products that aren't selling. You easily fall into "push to sell" mode instead of "sell what market demands". OEM allows nimble portfolio changes based on market signals.

5. Internal conflict between production and sales

In businesses with their own factory, it's not uncommon for production and sales teams to clash: production wants large batches for cost efficiency, sales wants small batches for order flexibility. This battle drains management energy in ways many CEOs don't anticipate.

Owning a factory isn't a sign of maturity — it's an asset class. This asset can increase business value, or become an operational burden, depending on whether you have sufficient volume and management capability. Interview with a domestic paint brand CEO

4. Four questions to answer before deciding

Q1: Is paint revenue stable, and at what level?

If revenue is below $3.2M/year and not yet stable, don't think about your own plant. The breakeven math doesn't support it. Focus on scaling sales and brand first.

Q2: Is the product formula proprietary enough to keep internal?

If the product uses industry-standard formulations, OEM serves it well. Own-plant only makes sense for unique IP that demands absolute confidentiality.

Q3: Do you have a production management team with 10+ years of experience?

Opening a plant without a strong Production Director is suicide. This talent is hard to recruit, well-paid ($30K-$70K USD/year), and rarely leaves established companies for a startup. This is the heaviest human capital constraint.

Q4: Is the long-term goal an exit (IPO/M&A)?

Investors sometimes value brands with their own factory higher because of tangible assets. But wise investors usually value by cash flow and EBITDA — tangible assets matter less. Consult an industry M&A expert before investing in a plant for this purpose.

5. Conclusion — no universal answer

The OEM-vs-own-factory decision is not a technical choice, but a strategic one. It depends on:

  • Volume and stability of revenue
  • Production management capability of the current team
  • Nature of the product (industry-standard vs proprietary formula)
  • The founder's 5-10 year vision

One of the best domestic paint brands we know operates a Hybrid model: produces 60% of core products in a small in-house plant, OEMs 40% of secondary SKUs. They retain the advantages of both models without the pressure of either.

Final advice: when uncertain, don't rush. One more year of OEM costs a few extra percentage points; a wrong factory costs millions and five years of recovery. The opportunity cost of a wrong decision in manufacturing always exceeds the opportunity cost of a slower one.
